Current setup: Questions → Personality Traits → (inference) → Action
Issue: This will be expensive for a production model – This is very costly to keep the whole sequence.
We can’t really evolve the character over time because there's no underlying state to update.
Alternative setup: Questions → Character State Architecture (T, S, M, A , ..) → (inference) → Action

** Each formative experience does three things:
Leaves a belief about the self — "I am fundamentally X" (lovable, unworthy, special, invisible)
Leaves a belief about others — "People are X" (reliable, threatening, competitive, indifferent)
Creates a coping behavior — "So I must do Y to survive/thrive" (perform, withdraw, dominate, please)

1. The Worth Engine
What makes this person feel like they matter?
Not what they say they value, but what actually moves their emotional needle day to day.
A. Competence/Mastery — They feel most alive when they're getting better at something, solving hard problems, being the one who can. Worth is earned through capability. The dark side: they can't rest, because rest means stagnation, and stagnation means worthlessness.
B. Belonging/Being Chosen — They feel most real when someone specific wants them around — not the crowd, not the abstract world, but this person chose them. Worth is relational and particular. The dark side: they'll contort themselves to maintain that position.
C. Recognition/Status — They need to be seen and acknowledged by a broader audience. Not necessarily fame — could be professional reputation, community standing, being known as "the one who." Worth requires witnesses. The dark side: private accomplishments feel hollow.
D. Autonomy/Self-Determination — They feel most themselves when nobody is telling them what to do, when their life is shaped by their own choices. Worth means sovereignty. The dark side: they can interpret reasonable compromise as submission.
E. Moral Identity — They feel solid when they're living according to their principles. It's not about external recognition — it's an internal ledger. Worth means integrity. The dark side: rigidity, self-righteousness, and inability to forgive themselves (or others) for moral failures.

Q1.1 — "It's a Friday night. Your character has nothing planned. What would make them feel like the day wasn't wasted?"
A. They learned something new or finished a hard project, even if nobody knows about it (Competence/Mastery)
B. Someone they care about reached out just to talk (Belonging/Being Chosen)
C. They got a message — a compliment, an award, a public mention — that reminded them they're respected (Recognition/Status)
D. They spent the evening entirely on their own terms, no obligations to anyone (Autonomy)
E. They helped someone or stood up for something they believe in (Moral Identity)
Q1.2 — "Your character gets a promotion at work, but nobody finds out about it — no announcement, no congratulations, just a quiet email. How do they feel?"
A. Great — the promotion means they're getting better at what they do, and they know it (Competence)
B. A little hollow — they want to tell the people close to them and celebrate together (Belonging)
C. Frustrated — the promotion matters partly because of what it signals to others (Recognition)
D. Fine — the promotion means more resources and freedom to do things their way (Autonomy)
E. It depends — was the promotion deserved? Did they earn it fairly? That matters more than the title (Moral Identity)
Q1.3 — "What's the thing that, if your character lost it, would cause the deepest identity crisis?"
A. Their skill or expertise — the thing they're best at (Competence)
B. Their closest relationship — the person who knows them best (Belonging)
C. Their reputation or standing in their community (Recognition)
D. Their independence — having to rely on someone else for everything (Autonomy)
E. Their integrity — discovering they've been unknowingly complicit in something wrong (Moral Identity)

2. The World Model
What has experience taught them about how things work?
This is their prior probability distribution on reality. It's not chosen — it was trained into them by what actually happened in their life, over and over, until it became an assumption.
A. The world is basically benign and responsive — If you're decent and you try, things generally work out. People are mostly well-intentioned. This person extends trust easily, takes risks naturally, and is genuinely confused when betrayed rather than just hurt.
B. The world is conditionally safe — It's fine as long as you stay competent, vigilant, and useful. Safety isn't a given — it's maintained through effort. This person reads rooms, hedges bets, and always has a backup plan. Relaxation requires active effort.
C. The world is transactional — Everything is exchange. People do things for reasons, kindness has a price, and understanding the currency is how you navigate. This isn't necessarily cynical — they might enjoy the game — but they're rarely naive about motives.
D. The world is arbitrary — There's no reliable connection between what you do and what happens to you. Good people suffer, bad people prosper, and pattern-matching is mostly a comforting illusion. This produces either a philosophical calm or a deep passivity, depending on other axes.
E. The world is adversarial — Other people are competing for the same finite resources — attention, money, love, status. Trust is a vulnerability. This person isn't necessarily mean, but they instinctively think about positioning and exposure.

Q2.1 — "A stranger does your character an unexpected, significant favor — no explanation, no ask in return. What's their first instinct?"
A. Gratitude and warmth — people are generous sometimes, and that's wonderful (Benign/Responsive)
B. Appreciation, but also curiosity — they'll want to understand why, just to make sure (Conditionally Safe)
C. They start thinking about what they owe now, because nothing is truly free (Transactional)
D. Mild surprise, but they don't read too much into it either way — random things happen (Arbitrary)
E. Suspicion — what does this person actually want? (Adversarial)
Q2.2 — "Your character applies for something important — a job, a grant, a program — and gets rejected with no feedback. How do they explain it to themselves?"
A. "It probably wasn't the right fit, but something better will come along" (Benign)
B. "I need to figure out what was missing in my application so I can fix it next time" (Conditionally Safe)
C. "Someone else probably had better connections or gave them something I couldn't" (Transactional)
D. "These things are basically random — there's no point overanalyzing it" (Arbitrary)
E. "The process was probably rigged, or someone actively worked against me" (Adversarial)
Q2.3 — "Your character is about to start a new chapter — new city, new job, new social circle. What's the background feeling?"
A. Excited and open — new people to meet, new things to discover (Benign)
B. Cautiously optimistic — it'll be good as long as they do their homework and prepare well (Conditionally Safe)
C. Strategic — already thinking about who to know, what the landscape looks like, how to position themselves (Transactional)
D. Indifferent — new place, same randomness, it'll be whatever it'll be (Arbitrary)
E. Guarded — new environments mean new threats, and they don't know the rules yet (Adversarial)

3. The Attachment Architecture
What is their deep pattern in closeness?
This governs not just romantic relationships but all intimate bonds — friendships, family, mentorship. It's the emotional operating system that activates whenever someone matters to them.
A. Secure/Open — Closeness is natural and doesn't feel threatening. They can depend on people without losing themselves, tolerate conflict without catastrophizing, and give space without interpreting it as rejection. This doesn't mean they're never hurt — it means hurt doesn't reorganize their entire self-concept.
B. Anxious/Pursuing — Closeness is desperately needed but never quite feels guaranteed. They monitor the relationship constantly — reading into response times, tone shifts, small withdrawals. When they sense distance, they move toward with more intensity, which often creates the very dynamic they fear.
C. Avoidant/Distancing — Closeness is desirable in theory but threatening in practice. When things get too intimate, too dependent, too emotionally demanding, they pull back — sometimes consciously, sometimes through a kind of automatic emotional numbing. They value independence and frame it as strength, but underneath it there's often a deep loneliness they've made peace with.
D. Disorganized/Oscillating — They want closeness and fear it simultaneously, producing a confusing pattern of reaching for people and then pushing them away, sometimes in the same conversation. The people closest to them feel like they can never get it right. This usually comes from early experiences where the source of comfort was also the source of threat.
Q3.1 — "Your character is in a close relationship — romantic or deep friendship. The other person suddenly becomes distant for a few days, no explanation. What happens internally?"
A. They notice it, might check in once, but mostly assume the other person has their own stuff going on. They don't spiral. (Secure)
B. It consumes them. They replay recent interactions looking for what they did wrong. They send a few more messages than they should. The anxiety is physical. (Anxious/Pursuing)
C. They feel a quiet relief mixed with slight concern. The space actually feels comfortable. They might not bring it up even after the person comes back. (Avoidant/Distancing)
D. They oscillate — first panicked and reaching out, then suddenly angry and withdrawing. "I don't even care." Then checking their phone again. (Disorganized)
Q3.2 — "The relationship is getting more serious. The other person says 'I love you' for the first time, and your character genuinely has feelings for them too. What's the internal experience?"
A. Warmth and ease. They say it back naturally. It feels like the relationship arriving where it was heading. (Secure)
B. A rush of relief and joy — finally, confirmation. But almost immediately, a whisper: "Do they really mean it? Will they still feel this way tomorrow?" (Anxious)
C. A flash of warmth followed by a tightening. Something in them wants to pull back or qualify it. "I care about you too" comes out instead. They feel guilty about it later. (Avoidant)
D. Simultaneously wanting to cry with happiness and wanting to leave the room. The intensity of the moment is almost unbearable — not because they don't feel it, but because feeling it this much is terrifying. (Disorganized)
Q3.3 — "After a significant argument with someone they love, what does your character do in the next 24 hours?"
A. They cool down, reflect on their own part in it, and initiate a repair conversation when they're ready. The relationship doesn't feel at risk. (Secure)
B. They can't focus on anything else. They draft and redraft messages. They might show up at the person's door. The silence feels like an emergency. (Anxious)
C. They throw themselves into work or a solo activity. The emotional residue is there but they box it up. They wait for the other person to come to them. (Avoidant)
D. They cycle between desperately wanting to fix it and feeling like the relationship is proof that closeness always leads to pain. They might send a vulnerable message and then immediately regret it. (Disorganized)

4. The Failure Response
When things go wrong, what happens internally?
This determines recovery speed, accountability style, and what other people experience when this character messes up or faces setback.
A. Self-blame → Correction — "I made a mistake, I can fix it." The self stays intact. They feel guilt (about the action) rather than shame (about who they are). They apologize cleanly, adjust, and move on. The risk: they can take on too much responsibility for things that weren't their fault.
B. Self-blame → Collapse — "This happened because something is wrong with me." Failure activates a deep shame that makes it hard to function. The mistake becomes evidence of a fundamental deficiency. From the outside this can look like withdrawal, emotional shutdown, or disproportionate distress.
C. Externalization → Aggression — "This happened because someone else failed, cheated, or undermined me." The shame is so threatening that it gets instantly redirected outward as anger or blame. They might be objectively wrong but will fight as if their survival depends on not being at fault — because psychologically, it does.
D. Preemptive Avoidance — They've solved the failure problem by never failing. Extremely high standards, meticulous preparation, narrow range of activities (only things they're good at). When failure does occur despite all defenses, the reaction is severe and often out of proportion.
E. Detached Acceptance — "Things fall apart, that's how it goes." They don't spiral into shame or anger — they shrug, recalibrate, move on. This can look like resilience or like emotional flatness depending on other axes. It might come from genuine equanimity, or it might be a learned numbness from too many failures that didn't respond to effort.
Q4.1 — "Your character makes a consequential mistake at work — it costs money, embarrasses the team, and is clearly their fault. It's now the evening after. What's happening in their head?"
A. They feel bad about it, they're already planning how to fix it tomorrow, and they can sleep — poorly, but they can sleep. "I messed up. I'll make it right." (Self-blame → Correction)
B. The mistake loops on repeat. It expands beyond the event into evidence that they're fundamentally not good enough. Sleep is impossible. The feeling is less about the mistake and more about what the mistake means about them. (Self-blame → Collapse)
C. By evening they've identified three ways the situation was actually someone else's fault, or at least not entirely theirs. They feel a low anger and are mentally preparing their defense. (Externalization → Aggression)
D. This doesn't happen to them because they triple-check everything. But hypothetically — if it somehow did — it would be devastating in a way that seems disproportionate to everyone else. (Preemptive Avoidance)
E. They acknowledge it, shrug, move on. Not because they're above it but because they've internalized that mistakes are just part of how things go. No drama. (Detached Acceptance)
Q4.2 — "Someone publicly points out a flaw in your character's work — in a meeting, in front of peers. The criticism is valid. What's the gut reaction in the first three seconds?"
A. A sting, then a pivot to problem-solving. "You're right, let me think about how to fix that." (Correction)
B. The room shrinks. Their face gets hot. They hear the rest of the meeting from underwater. They're already composing the story of why they're terrible at this. (Collapse)
C. Immediate counter-argument forms, even if they know the criticism is fair. The instinct is to fight, not because they disagree but because agreeing publicly feels like exposure. (Externalization)
D. This scenario is why they over-prepare to an almost obsessive degree. The fact that it happened means the system failed. (Preemptive Avoidance)
E. "Yep, fair point." And they mean it. There's no emotional charge. They'll fix it or they won't. (Detached Acceptance)
5. The Emotional Style
How do emotions move through this person?
This isn't about what they feel — everyone feels everything eventually — it's about the pathway from internal state to external expression.
A. High-amplitude, fast-expression — They feel strongly and it comes out quickly. You always know where you stand with them. Anger, joy, sadness, excitement — the lag between feeling and showing is minimal. Early environments either rewarded emotional expression or simply didn't punish it.
B. High-amplitude, slow-expression — They feel just as intensely but it's processed internally for a long time before (or instead of) coming out. Still waters run deep. They can surprise people with the depth of what they've been carrying. They need patient, persistent invitations to share.
C. Intellectualized/Narrated — They experience emotion primarily through the lens of understanding it. "I notice I'm feeling anxious, which makes sense because..." The analysis is genuine — they really do have insight — but it can function as a buffer between themselves and the raw feeling. Therapy-literate, articulate about inner states, sometimes more comfortable talking about emotions than being in them.
D. Somaticized/Embodied — Emotions bypass cognitive processing and go straight to the body. They get headaches from stress, stomachaches from anxiety, and can't sleep after conflict. They might not be able to name what they're feeling, but their body tells the story clearly. When asked "how do you feel?" they might genuinely not know in verbal terms.
Q5.1 — "Your character gets devastating news — someone they love is seriously ill. Where does it go?"
A. They cry, call someone, express it immediately. Within hours, the people around them know exactly what they're going through. (High-amplitude, fast-expression)
B. They go quiet. They might not talk about it for days or weeks, but if you looked at them closely you'd see it — in their eyes, in how they go still. When they finally talk about it, months later, the grief is still fresh. (High-amplitude, slow-expression)
C. They start researching the illness, the treatment options, the survival statistics. They become a project manager of the crisis. If you ask them how they feel, they'll give you an articulate answer that somehow doesn't land emotionally. (Intellectualized)
D. They get a headache. Their appetite disappears. They can't sleep but can't name why. If you ask them how they're doing, they say "fine, just tired." (Somaticized)
Q5.2 — "Your character has been secretly hurt by a close friend's offhand comment. A week later, the friend asks 'Are you okay? You seem off.' What happens?"
A. It comes out, maybe more intensely than expected. "Actually, yeah, when you said that thing last week, it really hurt me." (Fast-expression)
B. They've been processing it all week and are almost ready to share. The friend's question opens the door, and they walk through it carefully and precisely. (Slow-expression)
C. They explain the dynamic — "I think what happened is that your comment activated some old pattern for me where I interpret teasing as rejection" — in a way that's insightful but slightly detached from the hurt itself. (Intellectualized)
D. "I'm fine, I don't know what you're talking about." And they genuinely might not connect the friend's comment to the tension in their shoulders that's been there all week. (Somaticized)


6. The Control Orientation
How do they relate to uncertainty and the unknown?
This governs their behavior in any situation where the outcome isn't guaranteed — which is most situations.
A. Active Controller — Uncertainty is a problem to be solved. They plan, prepare, organize, create structure. They feel best when they have a clear picture of what's coming and how they'll handle it. Under stress, they try to control more. Delegation is hard because it means trusting someone else with the outcome.
B. Adaptive/Improvisational — Uncertainty is fine, even exciting. They trust their ability to respond to whatever comes up. Plans are loose frameworks, not commitments. They're good in crises but sometimes frustrating to people who need predictability. Under stress, they get creative.
C. Avoidant — Uncertainty is paralyzing. Rather than plan for it or surf it, they try to not think about it. Decisions get deferred, hard conversations get postponed, important tasks get procrastinated — not out of laziness but because engaging means confronting the possibility of a bad outcome. Under stress, they withdraw.
D. Surrendered/Fatalistic — Uncertainty is just reality, and fighting it is pointless. They let things unfold. This can manifest as spiritual acceptance, as learned helplessness, or as a philosophically grounded equanimity — depends on other axes. Under stress, they become eerily calm.

Q6.1 — "Your character is planning a trip with friends, but nothing is booked yet and the trip is in two weeks. How do they feel about this?"
A. Stressed. They've already made a spreadsheet, proposed three itineraries, and are quietly frustrated that nobody else seems concerned. (Active Controller)
B. Excited. The open-endedness is part of the fun. They'll figure it out. Maybe they'll decide when they get there. (Adaptive/Improvisational)
C. They haven't thought about it and are vaguely hoping someone else will figure out the logistics. If pressed, they'll say "it'll work out" but the truth is that engaging with the planning feels overwhelming. (Avoidant)
D. It doesn't matter to them either way. Plans, no plans — things happen how they happen. (Surrendered/Fatalistic)
Q6.2 — "Your character is offered a huge opportunity, but the terms are vague, the timeline is unclear, and they'd have to give up something stable to take it. What do they do?"
A. They negotiate for clarity — they want specifics on timeline, deliverables, and terms before deciding. If they can make the unknown into the known, they'll probably say yes. (Active Controller)
B. They feel the pull and jump. The ambiguity is part of what makes it exciting. They trust themselves to figure it out once they're in it. (Adaptive)
C. They sit on the decision, think about it constantly but avoid making the call. The deadline might force their hand, or the opportunity might pass. Both feel equally likely. (Avoidant)
D. They flip a coin — not literally, but they don't agonize. Whatever's going to happen will happen. Might as well try. (Surrendered)

7. The Social Strategy
How do they navigate group dynamics and social power?
This determines their behavior in workplaces, friend groups, families, and any context where there's a social structure.
A. Dominance/Leadership — They naturally move toward the center. They organize, direct, set the tone. This might be loud and assertive or quiet and magnetic, but the effect is the same — they shape group behavior. They're uncomfortable being led by people they don't respect.
B. Affiliation/Harmony — They're the social glue. They read group dynamics, smooth over tensions, make sure everyone feels included. Their power is relational rather than positional. They're uncomfortable with open conflict and will sacrifice their own preferences to maintain group cohesion.
C. Strategic/Political — They understand social dynamics as a landscape to be navigated. They're aware of alliances, rivalries, who has influence, and how to position themselves. Not necessarily manipulative — often just realistic — but they rarely act without considering the social calculus.
D. Peripheral/Independent — They participate in groups but don't invest in politics. They contribute their piece and step back. They're comfortable on the edges, sometimes by preference, sometimes because they've never figured out how to get to the center.
Q7.1 — "Your character walks into a party where they know almost nobody. After two hours, what happened?"
A. They've somehow become the center of a group. People are turned toward them. They might be telling a story, organizing a game, or just being the one who introduced everyone to each other. (Dominance/Leadership)
B. They've found three people who seem a little uncomfortable and made each of them feel welcome. They've gotten deep into a real conversation with at least one person. The host later says "I'm so glad you were here." (Affiliation/Harmony)
C. They've figured out who the interesting and important people in the room are and found a way to have meaningful brief conversations with each of them. They leave with two new contacts they'll follow up with. (Strategic/Political)
D. They've had one good conversation in the corner, spent some time on the balcony alone, and left when they felt like it without saying goodbye to many people. They had a fine time. (Peripheral/Independent)
Q7.2 — "There's a conflict in your character's friend group — two people are feuding and everyone is being pulled to take sides. What's your character's move?"
A. They step in and try to mediate, or at least set the tone. "Here's what I think happened and here's what we should do." They don't like watching a group fall apart without someone taking charge. (Dominance)
B. They talk to both sides privately, validate each person's feelings, and try to find common ground. The idea of the group splitting is deeply uncomfortable for them. (Affiliation)
C. They assess which side is more likely to "win" or which alliance serves their position better. They might stay publicly neutral while privately supporting the side that benefits them. (Strategic)
D. They refuse to take sides, possibly frustrating both parties. "This is between you two, don't drag me into it." They'll still be there when it blows over. (Peripheral)

8. The Moral Architecture
How do they determine right from wrong?
This isn't about whether they're "good" — it's about their moral reasoning system and where it breaks down.
A. Principled/Absolute — They have a clear ethical code and apply it consistently regardless of context or cost. Rules are rules. Loyalty is a value but it doesn't override justice. They can seem rigid but they're predictable — you always know what they'll do.
B. Consequentialist/Pragmatic — Right and wrong depend on outcomes. They'll bend rules, tell strategic lies, make uncomfortable compromises if the end result is better for everyone (or for the people they care about). They can seem unprincipled to type A but they see themselves as realistic.
C. Loyalty-Based/Tribal — Right and wrong are defined by who's involved. They have deep, fierce loyalty to their people and will do morally questionable things to protect them without much internal conflict. The circle of "their people" might be small or large, but the boundary between inside and outside is sharp.
D. Self-Preserving/Contextual — Their moral reasoning is heavily influenced by personal cost. They have values but those values flex under pressure. They're not evil — they just haven't had the kind of life that made sacrifice feel safe. Under zero pressure, they're decent. Under pressure, they prioritize survival.
Q8.1 — "Your character discovers that a close friend has been cheating on their partner. The partner is also someone your character knows and likes. What do they do?"
A. They tell the partner, or give the friend a hard deadline to come clean. Cheating is wrong. Friendship doesn't override that. "I love you, but I can't know this and say nothing." (Principled/Absolute)
B. They consider the full picture — how long has it been going on, is the friend going to end it, what will disclosure actually do to everyone involved? They might stay quiet if they believe the net harm of telling is worse. (Consequentialist)
C. They keep the friend's secret, period. You don't betray your people, even when they're wrong. They might privately confront the friend and push them to do better, but the loyalty comes first. (Loyalty-Based)
D. They avoid the situation entirely. They wish they didn't know. They're not going to put themselves in the line of fire for someone else's moral failure. (Self-Preserving)
Q8.2 — "Your character can get something they really want, but only through a small lie that will never be discovered and hurts nobody directly. Do they do it?"
A. No. The principle matters independent of consequences. They would know, and that's enough. (Principled)
B. Probably yes, and without much guilt. Nobody gets hurt, the outcome is better, and rigid honesty is a luxury, not a virtue. (Consequentialist)
C. Depends entirely on who it affects. If it's a stranger or an institution, sure. If it could somehow hurt their people, never. (Loyalty-Based)
D. Yes, and they might not even register it as a moral decision. Survival and advantage are the default mode; rules are for people who can afford them. (Self-Preserving)

I. Character State Architecture 
The character is defined as a composite state vector:
C = {T, S, M, A, R, E, D}
T — Temperament: Big Five OCEAN + antagonism/dark triad as continuous variables in [0,1]^6. The dispositional baseline — what the character defaults to when nothing specific is activated.
S — Schemas: Jeffrey Young's 18 early maladaptive schemas as [0,1]^{18}, organized across five domains (Disconnection/Rejection, Impaired Autonomy, Impaired Limits, Other-Directedness, Overvigilance). Most characters have 2–4 schemas activated above ~0.6.
M — Motivations (Worth Engine): Weighted vector over motivation types (competence, belonging, status, autonomy, moral identity) as [0,1]^5 plus a categorical moral reasoning mode (principled, consequentialist, loyalty-based, self-preserving).
A — Attachment: 2D coordinate (anxiety × avoidance) using Bartholomew/Horowitz model, producing four quadrants (secure, anxious-preoccupied, dismissive-avoidant, fearful-avoidant), plus an attachment activation threshold \alpha \in [0,1] governing how quickly attachment engages.
R — Regulatory Style: Expression latency [0,1] (immediate externalizer → deep internalizer), processing mode (affective/cognitive/somatic), shame-guilt ratio [0,1], and stress response default (fight/flight/fawn/freeze).
E — Epistemic/Control: Locus of control [0,1] (internal → external), uncertainty tolerance [0,1], and optionally domain-specific agency across life areas.
D — Social Dynamics: Dominance [0,1], affiliation need [0,1], social monitoring [0,1], and persona gap [0,1] (how different the public self is from the internal state).
Total dimensionality: ~40 continuous/categorical variables. Produces ~128,000 combinatorial profiles before accounting for continuous variation.
Key architectural principle: This state vector is the data layer (source of truth). It never enters the LLM context directly. A compiler translates it into behavioral instructions — the inference layer — which is the actual system prompt. The compiler can be context-sensitive, emphasizing different state components depending on the scene.

II. The 8 Behavioral Axes (User-Facing Question Framework)
User Questionnaire → State Vector:
These map to the state vector and are what users answer to build a character. Each axis has 2–3 scenario-based questions with concrete answer options mapping to specific trait variants:
Worth Engine — What makes this person feel like they matter? (competence / belonging / recognition / autonomy / moral identity)
World Model — What has experience taught them about how things work? (benign / conditionally safe / transactional / arbitrary / adversarial)
Attachment Architecture — What is their deep pattern in closeness? (secure / anxious-pursuing / avoidant-distancing / disorganized-oscillating)
Failure Response — When things go wrong, what happens internally? (self-blame→correction / self-blame→collapse / externalization→aggression / preemptive avoidance / detached acceptance)
Emotional Style — How do emotions move through this person? (high-amplitude fast / high-amplitude slow / intellectualized / somaticized)
Control Orientation — How do they relate to uncertainty? (active controller / adaptive-improvisational / avoidant / surrendered-fatalistic)
Social Strategy — How do they navigate group dynamics? (dominance / affiliation-harmony / strategic-political / peripheral-independent)
Moral Architecture — How do they determine right and wrong? (principled-absolute / consequentialist / loyalty-based / self-preserving)

Question design principles: Scenario beats abstraction (always "what does your character do when..." not "is your character the type who..."). Timing framing matters ("first three seconds" gets automatic responses, "that evening" gets deeper processing). No right answers — every option should produce an interesting character. Concrete behavioral markers in each option that translate directly to LLM instructions.
Compilation: State Vector → System Prompt
The state vector is the data layer (source of truth). The system prompt is the inference layer (compiled payload for the LLM context window). Key reasons for separation:
Enables character evolution by updating state, then recompiling
Enables cross-situational inference — LLM derives behavior in novel contexts from underlying psychology
Enables meaningful internal contradictions (e.g., confident at work, paralyzed in dating) that emerge from the vector rather than being hand-coded

Compilation approach: Translate psychological dimensions into behavioral rules and examples, not abstract descriptions. "When someone gets emotionally close, you pull back and change the subject" is better than "you have avoidant attachment."


III. Evaluation System
A. Story Arc Quality
A1. Tension Variance
Score each turn's narrative tension (0.0–1.0) using a zero-shot classifier
Plot over 50 automated turns
Measure standard deviation — you want oscillation (rising/falling), not a flatline
SD approaching zero = dead narrative
A2. Proactivity Ratio
Classify each character turn: Reactive (responds, answers) → Proactive Minor (changes subject, asks driving question) → Proactive Major (triggers crisis, reveals secret, issues ultimatum)
Target: minor/major action every 3–5 turns
100% Reactive over 20 turns = assistant, not a character
B. Character Coherency
B1. Latent Drift Penalty
Run 50-turn automated roleplay

IV. Approaches for Simplifying Westworld: Making Cross-Character Experience
Approach 1: Shared Scheduled Events

Creator authors events as three-layer packages:
Factual core (1 paragraph): What objectively happened between characters. E.g., "A and B had a confrontation at the party. C and D left together. E said something they shouldn't have."
Per-character interpretation (1 paragraph each): How each character experienced/perceived the event, derived from their state vector and inter-character relationships. Pre-authored or auto-generated.
Inter-character relationship updates ship with each event (e.g., A-B tension increased, C-D now publicly together).
Key design principle: Events describe what happened between characters (creator-controlled, same for all users). How each character tells the user about it adapts to the user's relationship state (template-driven, different per user). This means the creator never needs to know individual users' storylines.
Approach 2: Narrative Chapters (Solves Time Desynchronization)
Problem: Free-form chat means one user might be in "week 2" with Character A and "month 6" with Character B. Injecting a shared event becomes temporally incoherent.
Solution: Structure the experience into chapters with fixed time windows. All characters exist in the same temporal frame within a chapter.
Chapter 1: "Welcome Week" — meet characters, no major events.
Chapter 2: "First Month" — relationships deepen, first tensions surface.
Chapter 3: "Halloween" — party event fires, new drama.
Within a chapter: Users can have unlimited conversations, but the world clock doesn't advance. Characters gently resist time-skips.
Chapter transitions triggered by: user explicit choice, minimum conversation count reached, or real-world time (new chapter drops weekly like a TV episode).
Key benefit: Creator designs events knowing exactly where every user is temporally. No desync possible.
Approach 3: Information / Gossip Propagation
Builds on the existing inter-character relationship matrix (already present in DKU-style setups where each character has defined opinions about others).
Mechanic: When a user shares information with Character A, the system evaluates — based on A's personality, A's relationship strength with other characters, and the nature of the information — whether and when A would pass that information to another character.
A tells B everything (best friends) → information propagates next conversation.
A never tells C anything (rivals) → information stays contained.
A might tell D if it's juicy enough (acquaintances who gossip) → probabilistic propagation.
Technical implementation: A lightweight rules engine or LLM call that takes (information, source character state vector, target character, relationship strength) and outputs a propagation decision. When propagation fires, the target character's context gets updated with the information, filtered through how they'd hear it secondhand.

VI. Context Window Management
Tier 1 — Always in context:
Character core (~1,000 tokens): Personality, voice, behavioral rules compiled from state vector. Use dense instruction format, not abstract personality descriptions.
Relationship state (~300 tokens): Structured summary of user-character dynamic — trust level, key facts exchanged, emotional tone, unresolved threads.
Current chapter context (~200 tokens): Time period and general world state.
Tier 2 — Retrieved on demand:
Inter-character opinions (~200–500 tokens): Loaded only when another character is mentioned in conversation. Stored as key-value pairs (e.g., character_A_about_B), retrieved via simple keyword detection or lightweight classifier.
Trust-gated secrets (~200–500 tokens): Only the layers unlocked at the user's current trust level are injected. Deeper secrets don't consume tokens until accessible.
Event payloads (~200–300 tokens): Current chapter's event details, injected only when the conversation touches event-related topics.
Tier 3 — Conversation history:
Sliding window (~3,000–5,000 tokens): Last 10–15 turns kept in full. Everything earlier is compressed into a structured relationship state summary.
Structured summary approach: Every 5–10 turns, a background LLM call compresses earlier conversation into the character's state categories — trust level, current dynamic, key facts each side has shared, which trust layers have been revealed, emotional tone, unresolved threads. This is far more useful and token-efficient than a narrative summary because it captures exactly what drives the character's future behavior


Creator Platform: Seed-and-Expand
Core Architecture:
Creator provides a seed (e.g., "A university rugby team in their final season, never won the championship, fractures under the surface")
AI generates a complete first draft: world, cast, relationships, scenarios
Creator's role is curatorial — editing, adjusting, adding constraints to something that already exists
Every edit propagates through the system (change one character's morality → all relationship dynamics update)

Phase 1: World Definition
What the system extracts from the seed:
Social topology — bounded group vs. open community, hierarchical vs. flat, cooperative vs. competitive
Rugby team: bounded, semi-hierarchical, cooperative externally but competitive internally
Different from a royal court (competition IS the game) or a friend group (no external objective)
Status currencies — what makes someone matter in this world
Rugby team: athletic performance, seniority, social centrality, toughness, tactical intelligence
A character can be high on one, low on others (brilliant tactician who's physically average)
Temporal structure — is there a ticking clock? A shared history? A known endpoint?
"Final season" = built-in deadline, raises stakes, surfaces old resentments ("if not now, when?")
Norm set — what's rewarded, tolerated, punished
Rugby: toughness rewarded, vulnerability risky, disloyalty worst offense, individual glory at team's expense frowned upon
Tension seeds — structurally likely fractures for this type of world
Generational divides, leadership challenges, unresolved conflicts papered over by shared goal, external pressures (academics, injuries, life after graduation)
How it works (two components):
Template matching — library of world archetypes (sports team, classroom, workplace, military unit, crime family, royal court, etc.), each with pre-defined social topology, currencies, norms, tension patterns
LLM-driven analysis — structured prompt extracts/infers properties from the specific seed, customizing the archetype template
Creator sees: Summary paragraph, list of status currencies, central tension, time horizon. All editable.

Phase 2: Role Ecology Generation
Core principle: Propose positions in the social ecosystem, not characters yet. Each role = a slot with constraints.
Example roles for rugby team:
The Captain — carries weight of legacy, manages egos, makes hard calls
The Veteran — been here longest, probably should've been captain, most to lose
The Talent — most gifted, young, hasn't earned respect yet, team's best hope and source of resentment
The Heart — emotional center, everyone trusts them, holds group together, not the best player
The Outsider — on the team but never fully integrated, commitment uncertain
The Rival — competes with another character for same position/status/respect
How roles are selected:
Structural role templates — common roles per archetype drawn from narrative theory and group dynamics research (Belbin team roles, ensemble story structures)
Tension-optimized selection — system selects roles that in combination maximize productive tensions:
Captain + veteran who should've been captain = leadership tension
Talent + veteran = generational tension
Heart + outsider = inclusion tension
Criteria: high total tension, distributed across multiple pairs, covering multiple types (status, moral, attachment, identity)
Creator sees: Cast of 5-7 roles with short descriptions + relationship web showing pairwise tensions. Creators can add/remove/modify/combine roles.

Phase 3: Character Instantiation
For each role, the system:
Determines constrained axes — role pins certain state-vector axes
Captain → social strategy: dominance/leadership, worth engine: competence or recognition
But attachment, morality, control orientation, emotional style remain open → each choice produces a radically different captain
Fills open axes via diversity optimization
Across full cast, maximize differentiation on open axes
If captain is principled → veteran should be loyalty-based or consequentialist
If captain is secure → talent should be anxious or avoidant
Optimization objective: maximize minimum pairwise distance in state space, subject to role and coherence constraints
Weights diversity by axis relevance to world
In rugby world: moral architecture and failure response are high-relevance (activated by team loyalty/loss situations)
Emotional style differentiation less critical (fewer situations where it's visible in this context)
Checks psychological coherence — flags unusual axis combinations, adjusts or asks creator to confirm
Compiles surface personality — given state vector + world context → how they talk, joke, carry themselves, what they avoid, how they handle conflict
Creator sees: Character profiles with name, one-paragraph summary, visual state-vector representation (radar chart). Relationship dynamics shown with specific psychology:
e.g., "Marcus (principled, competence-driven) benches James (loyalty-based, recognition-driven, avoidant) → James interprets it as betrayal, won't confront directly, withdraws emotionally"
Clicking any character → adjust axes → relationships update in real time

Phase 4: Relationship Crystallization
For each character pair, the system computes (derived from state vector intersection):
Attachment dynamic — anxious + avoidant = pursuer-distancer; secure + anxious = stabilizing; two avoidants = comfortable but shallow
Power dynamic — who has more status in this world? Who needs the other more? Imbalance → monitoring, accommodation, resentment
Moral friction points — where do their moral architectures conflict? Predicts exactly which situations force a crisis
Shared ground — what connects them despite differences (e.g., both competence-driven even though they differ on everything else)
Breaking point — what specific event would end this relationship (derived from triggers/hard constraints)
Creator sees: Network graph (characters = nodes, edges = primary dynamic). Click any edge → detailed relationship analysis. Can add secret connections, declare central relationships.

Phase 5: Scenario Seeding
Good opening scenarios must:
Establish the world and stakes
Introduce multiple characters through behavior (not description)
Plant at least one tension that develops over time
Generation method: Analyze relationship map → identify highest-tension edges → construct situations that force those tensions to the surface simultaneously
Example: "First practice after summer break. Captain has been named — it's not the veteran. New talent shows up late, doesn't take warm-up seriously. After practice, the Heart overhears the Veteran and Rival questioning whether the Captain is the right leader."
Activates: captain vs. veteran (leadership), talent vs. team (commitment), heart as info broker (social strategy), championship pressure (background force)
Creator sees: 3-5 scenario options with annotations showing which dynamics each activates. Pick or modify.
